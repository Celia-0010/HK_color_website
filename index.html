Automated
Urban Color Palette Generation

Using Google Street View Imagery
and Vision-Language Models

Urban color is a key component in shaping the visual perception and identity of citizens, yet remains insufficiently quantified in large-scale urban analytics.

We propose a systematic and scalable framework for generating urban color palettes by leveraging Google Street View imagery and Vision-Language models.

75,852 GSV images cross Hong Kong are processed using CLIP for zero-shot weather classification and Grounded SAM for open-vocabulary semantic segmentation.

Our findings reveal distinct spatial patterns in urban color that reflect regional characteristics, and strong explanatory power of urban color features in housing price prediction.

Representative Colors of Hong Kong

Combining Incremental K-means clustering in the RGB color space
and Color Difference Formula in the CIELab color space,
we extract 22 representative colors of Hong Kong from all targeted pixels.

Urban Color
Affected by Weather

CLIP is used to classify GSV images according to weather conditions.

On cloudy days, muted-neutral and dark-earthy colors are more dominant, due to diffuse lighting.

On sunny days, soft-cool and light-vibrant colors are more prominent, enhanced by direct sunlight.

Urban Color
Shaped by Semantics

Grounded SAM is used to segment GSV images into 18 semantic classes. Each pixel is assigned a semantic label, representing "what it is", and a color label, representing "what it looks like". This enables us to identify colors of specific semantics, as well as determine which semantics contribute to the presence of a color.

Artificial Background: low-saturation browns and greys, reflecting differences in material, age, or lighting exposure.

Natural Background: light blue or nearly white colors intrinsically from the sky, and green tones intrinsically from vegetation and terrain.

Static Decoration: greater color diversity with warm accent colors such as reds, oranges, and yellows, intended to attract attentions.

Dynamic Decoration: the greatest hue variation and highest saturation, reflecting personal choices like clothing and vehicle paint.

Spatial Heterogeneity of Urban Color

We perform clustering analysis based on the pixel ratios of 22 colors across all images.
Six dominant clusters are identified, demonstrating clear statistical and spatial patterns.

Predicting Housing Prices Using Urban Color Information

To assess the effectiveness of urban color features under controlled lighting conditions,
we conduct a housing price prediction task using only cloudy-day images with random forest regression.

Model performance improves as buffer size increases from 200m to 1000m, with 800m being optimal to capture the relationship.

The model with color features alone achieves an R² of 0.5553, outperforming the 0.5059 with semantic features alone.

Adding color features increases the R² of the base model with housing location and accessibility from 0.6085 to 0.6253.
